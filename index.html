Method by Scott Winship · Reanalysis applied to New York City

New York's neighborhoods by income, 1970 vs. 2015-2019

On April 30, 2026, the American Enterprise Institute's Scott Winship reanalyzed a widely cited University of Illinois Chicago Voorhees Center study and showed that Chicago's "disappearing middle class" actually moved up: the city's middle-income neighborhoods shrank because so many of them became upper-middle and upper-income. This page applies Winship's method, unchanged, to the five boroughs of New York City.

Full credit to Scott Winship and the American Enterprise Institute. The analytical method, income thresholds, inflation index, and framing all come from Winship's article "Chicago's 'Disappearing Middle Class' Can Be Found in Its Proliferating Upper Middle-Class Neighborhoods" (AEI, April 30, 2026), which itself builds on the Voorhees Center's tract classification scheme. This page contributes only the New York data and the charts. Any errors in the New York extension are mine.

Methodology, data sources, assumptions, and limitations

What this replicates

This page reproduces, for the five boroughs of New York City, the analytical approach used by Scott Winship of the American Enterprise Institute to reanalyze the University of Illinois Chicago Voorhees Center's "disappearing middle class" finding. Source article: Chicago's 'Disappearing Middle Class' Can Be Found in Its Proliferating Upper Middle-Class Neighborhoods (AEI, April 30, 2026).

Data sources

  • 1970 tract data: Brown University's Longitudinal Tract Data Base (LTDB) Standard Sample file for 1970, normalized to 2010 census tract boundaries. Variables used: POP70SP1 (sample population), INCPC70 (per-capita income, 1970 dollars). Source: s4.ad.brown.edu/projects/diversity/Researcher/LTDB.htm.
  • Recent tract data: Brown LTDB Standard Sample file based on the American Community Survey 2015-2019 5-year estimates, also on 2010 boundaries. Variables: pop19 (population), incpc19 (per-capita income, 2019 dollars). Winship's Chicago piece used the 2020-2024 ACS converted to 2010 boundaries via the Census Bureau crosswalk; that file requires a separate crosswalk step and was not used here so that the two endpoints share boundary geography without further interpolation. The direction of the result is robust to this choice.
  • Geography: All census tracts in the five New York City counties: Bronx (FIPS 36005), Kings/Brooklyn (36047), New York/Manhattan (36061), Queens (36081), Richmond/Staten Island (36085). 2,123 tracts in 1970 and 2,117 in 2015-2019, both on 2010 boundaries.
  • Metro reference: The 2010-defined New York-Newark-Jersey City Combined Statistical Area / Core-Based Statistical Area (CBSA 35620) tracts in the LTDB 1970 sample, used to compute the 1970 metro per-capita-income anchor.
  • Inflation: Bureau of Economic Analysis Personal Consumption Expenditures price index, annual averages on a 2017=100 chain-weighted basis. 1970 = 21.219, 2019 = 105.272. Multiplier 1970-to-2019 = 4.962.

Income bands

Following the original Voorhees Center scheme that Winship adopted and held constant in real terms, tracts are classified by population-weighted per-capita income relative to four thresholds, each pinned to a percentage of the 1970 metropolitan-area per-capita income:

The 1970 metropolitan-area per-capita income, computed as a population-weighted average across Brown LTDB tracts in the New York-Newark-Jersey City CBSA, is shown above. Thresholds are 60%, 80%, 120%, and 140% of that anchor.

Calculation

  1. For each New York City tract in 1970 and in 2015-2019, look up per-capita income.
  2. Compare to the four thresholds expressed in the same year's dollars (1970 dollars for 1970, 2019 dollars for 2015-2019).
  3. Assign tract to one of five bands (very low, low, middle, high, very high).
  4. Sum tract population to get the share of New York City residents in each band, citywide and by borough.

Assumptions and limitations

  • Per-capita income, not household income. This is a tract-level measure that mixes the income of working-age adults with that of children and retirees. It is what the Voorhees Center used and what Winship used; an analysis based on median household income would tell a related but not identical story.
  • Neighborhoods, not households. A "very-high-income tract" is one whose average resident has high income; it does not mean every household there is wealthy. Long-tenured low-income households can live in tracts that have moved up.
  • Endpoint comparison. The story between 1970 and the late 2010s was not monotonic. This file has only two snapshots.
  • 2010 boundary harmonization. Brown LTDB interpolates 1970 data onto 2010 tracts; that interpolation introduces error in tracts where boundaries shifted substantially.
  • 5-year ACS smoothing. The 2015-2019 incomes are 5-year averages, so they reflect mid-2017 conditions, not a single year.
  • Inflation index choice. Following Winship, this page uses the PCE deflator. Using the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U) would shift thresholds modestly higher and reduce the share gain at the top end; the direction of the result is robust to the choice.
  • Per-capita-income tracts in 1970 metro reference. A small number of 1970 LTDB tracts are missing per-capita income; those are excluded from the metro anchor calculation. The remaining sample covers more than 99% of population.

Reproducibility
